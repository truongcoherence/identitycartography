# Structuria Publication & Anonymization Standard — Working Standard

**Status:** Formalized July 12, 2026, in direct response to a demonstrated problem, not a theoretical one — a real, non-consenting third party's disclosure surfaced within minutes of testing an external data source, and a subsequent internal review found the same structural problem already present throughout this corpus's own source cases. Governs what may move from the internal working record into any public-facing document, including future OSF and GitHub material.

---
## 1. Rationale

This project has consistently anonymized its author. It has not, until now, had a formal standard for anonymizing everyone else who appears inside his stories.

Those are different operations. Removing the author's name from a case does not anonymize his father's words, a friend's exact phrasing, a mentor figure's absence, or a patient who never had any opportunity to consent to anything. Every one of those people is a non-consenting third party with respect to this project, structurally identical to the non-consenting third party discovered inside an external archive earlier this session — the only difference is that the ones already in this corpus were found by looking inward rather than outward.

A second problem compounds the first: **the cases carrying the most evidentiary weight in this corpus are also the ones carrying the highest exposure risk.** This is not a coincidence. A case is only checkable — only real Tier 2 evidence rather than vague gesture — because it retains specific, quoted, lived detail. Vague cases are safe and evidentially useless for the same reason. Any publication standard that simply strips detail until a case feels safe will also strip the case of the thing that made it worth citing. This standard exists to resolve that tension honestly rather than pretend it doesn't exist.

---

## 2. Who Governs Consent

**The narrator's consent covers what happened to them and the choices they made. It does not extend to other people's words, actions, or identifying details, no matter how minor their role or how directly the narrator experienced the events.**

This has one direct consequence worth stating plainly: the author's discretion over his own disclosures (his own termination, his own vulnerability, his own history) is his alone to exercise, and nothing in this standard restricts it. What this standard governs is everyone *else* who appears inside those disclosures — people who did not choose to be material in someone else's project.

Three distances of third-party involvement, requiring different handling:

- **Direct interlocutor** — a person who did something within the narrator's own witnessed event (a parent reacting, a mentor figure withdrawing, a friend responding). Present in the story as an active participant.

- **Present but peripheral** — identifiable but not the load-bearing content of the case (a colleague named in passing, an institution's staff generally).

- **Nested, non-participant** — a person who appears only because someone else's story passed through them, with no connection to this project at any remove (the clearest example: a disclosure made to a first responder, discovered inside an archived interview). This category receives the strictest handling in §7.

---

## 3. The Recognizability Test

Before any case moves from the internal working record toward public form, it must pass one question, applied to every third party in it individually, not to the case as a whole:

**Would this person recognize themselves in this account — or would someone who knows them recognize them — as it is currently written?**

If yes, the case is not ready, regardless of whether the author's own name has been removed. This test is deliberately more demanding than a generic anonymization pass. It is not asking whether a stranger could identify the person. It is asking whether *someone who already knows the story from the inside* would recognize it — the actual, realistic exposure risk, since the people most likely to encounter a published case involving them are the people who were already there.

---
## 4. Exposure Tiers

Applied retroactively below to the existing corpus as a first pass, and going forward to every new case at intake, not after the fact.

**Tier A — Low exposure.** Institutional or structural cases with no single third party centrally featured or directly quoted. Example: the SCM tagging case, the CT/Engineering scope gap, the RPIW authority case. Real workplaces, no individual whose specific words are the load-bearing content.

**Tier B — Medium exposure.** Real, potentially identifiable third parties present, but the case does not hinge on a personally sensitive disclosure from them. Example: the TCF/husband placement case, the deferred-resignation coworker, the fifty-year operator, the OR-project Convergence Point case. Lower personal stakes than Tier C, but the people involved still became data without being asked.

**Tier C — High exposure.** A direct interlocutor's specific, personally significant words or actions are the case's central evidentiary content. Example: the relationship case sourcing Boundaries (d) and (e); the coming-out case; the pregnant-friend case; the Peril genesis case, which is very likely the single highest-exposure item in the entire corpus — a real doctor, a real mentor figure, and a real patient, in an incident specific enough that colleagues from that period could plausibly reconstruct it even without names.

Tier C is not a disqualification. It is a flag that these cases require the full transformation process in §6 before they can move outward, not a light copyedit.

---
## 5. The Three-Layer Publication Model

This corpus already has two of the three layers this standard requires; it has been missing the middle one.

1. **Internal working record** — full specificity, complete provenance notes, real enough to remain checkable. What exists today for every restored IDP and the running Meta-Architecture document. Never leaves this layer as-is.
2. **Public canon** *(in progress)* — the actual physics: invariants, constraints, distortions, with source cases present but transformed until they pass the Recognizability Test. This is the missing layer, and building it is the actual publication task — not a copyedit of the internal documents, a distinct document per domain.
3. **Public pedigree** — already built (`Structuria - Pedigree and Development History.md`). High-level maturity and status, no case-level detail at all. Already safe as written, precisely because it never descended to the level of specificity that creates exposure.

## 7. Non-Negotiable

**Nested, non-participant third parties are not published, in any transformed or composited form, without a separate and specific decision to do so.** The person who exists in a story only because someone else's account passed through them was never in a position to consent to anything this project does, at any distance. Default is exclusion, not transformation.

---

## 8. Standing Rule Going Forward

Every new source case gets tiered under §4 at the moment it enters the internal working record, not retroactively when publication is being considered. This is now a required field alongside the Evidentiary Tier System's existing labels (Tier 1–4, cued/uncued) — every provenance table entry going forward should carry an exposure tier the same way it already carries an evidentiary tier.

## 10. Pre-Publication Mechanical Check

**Added July 12, 2026, after a named third-party identifier was tiered as Tier C, correctly flagged as needing anonymization, and then left in the document text anyway — caught only by a manual search after the fact.** The failure was not a lapse in attention during drafting. It was relying on attention during drafting at all, for a task that should never depend on it. This section exists so the check is mechanical and repeatable — run against every document before it moves toward public form, regardless of who or what produced the draft:

1. **Search for every specific name that has appeared anywhere in this project's field notes, Fieldwork Index entries, or prior conversation** — not just the obvious ones. A running list of names encountered so far should be maintained specifically for this search, since a name only has to be missed once to defeat a memory-based check.
2. **Search for specific institution names, site names, and program names** tied to Tier C cases, not just people's names.
3. **Cross-check every Tier C and Tier B case against Publication Standard §6** — has a transformation technique actually been applied, or only assigned as a tier label without the corresponding rewrite?
4. **Run this check as a literal search, not a read-through.** A read-through is the same vigilance-dependent process that already failed once.

This list is expected to grow. Add to it whenever a new category of identifier is discovered, the same way it just did.
