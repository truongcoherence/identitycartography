# Structuria StoryCorps Ingestion Policy

**Status:** July 12, 2026. Formalizes the author's proposed method. 
**Terms-of-use status: unresolved, flagged as a gate on this policy's execution, not a settled precondition.** See §4 before beginning sustained ingestion.

---

## 1. Method

Systematic archive traversal, not search-term selection — [StoryCorps Archive](https://archive.storycorps.org/), Interviews section, starting from the end of the listing and proceeding in order. One interview reviewed per day, read in full rather than scanned, evaluated against the Case Validity Checklist. If it passes, the transcript is logged for processing. If it fails, the day ends without a substitute — no reading further in search of one that fits.

**Calibrated July 13, 2026, based on a real result, not a hypothetical concern.** StoryCorps interviews commonly run 30 minutes and contain several loosely connected narrative threads, not one clean, self-contained story. The original method's implicit unit — "one candidate story" — didn't specify what to do when an interview contains multiple candidates, and a fast scan used to hand-pick the most promising-looking thread produced a segment that failed the Checklist's structural validity gate outright, a failure the scan itself did not catch. Faster and less precise turned out to mean exactly that in practice, not just in principle. The unit of "one per day" is therefore the interview, not a hand-picked segment within it — read in full, with every plausible thread tested against the Checklist before moving to the next interview. This will commonly yield zero usable cases from a given interview. That is an expected, normal outcome, not a failed day, consistent with this project's standing position that there is no need to rush data collection ahead of what the evidence actually supports.

## 2. Why This Rate, Explicitly, Not Just as a Constraint

Deliberately slow relative to the archive's actual size, by design rather than by limitation alone. Two independent reasons, both real: it matches what the author's own primary employment can sustain without this project displacing it, and it reflects the Limitations and Invitation to Extend document's own stated position — the physics need more data, but honest disclosure of current confidence does not require rushing to "stabilize the structure" before it's earned. Sustainable pace is the correct pace, not a compromise on rigor.

## 3. How This Satisfies the Case Validity Checklist

Sequential, ordered traversal is the checklist's _preferred_ method over search-term selection (§1: "where systematic archive traversal is possible... prefer it over search-term-based selection entirely") — this plan already meets the stronger standard, not a fallback one. The one-per-day cap functions as a pre-committed batch size in the strictest possible form. Priming risk from the author's own life is not present here in the way it is for personally-sourced cases, since candidates arrive in a fixed, externally-determined order rather than being chosen from memory.

## 4. Terms-of-Use Gate — Not Yet Cleared

StoryCorps states directly that it _"supports the use of its materials by researchers."_ A separate StoryCorps terms-of-use document (for a different product, not confirmed to govern the archive itself) restricts copying, downloading, and derivative use of platform content. The archive's own terms of use, as retrieved, did not clearly resolve which standard applies to a third party reading and logging transcripts for private research rather than uploading content. **Recommended before sustained logging begins:** a short, direct inquiry to StoryCorps' research contact, confirming that reading archived interviews and logging structural notes for private, non-commercial research is within scope. This is narrower and lower-cost than the formal research-proposal submission already planned for the publication stage, and resolves a real present uncertainty rather than an eventual one. Until answered, treat logged transcripts as provisional — usable for internal methodology testing, not yet cleared for anything moving toward public canon.

## 5. Naming and Anonymization

Per this project's own prior finding: StoryCorps participants are not anonymous — real names are standard. Any case drawn from this source that clears the Case Validity Checklist and enters the Case Registry is Tier B or higher by default, never Tier A, until proven otherwise on a case-by-case basis, and subject to the full Publication & Anonymization Standard before any public use.

## 6. Publication Gate — A Separate Commitment From Initial Research Access

**§4's terms-of-use question and this gate are not the same permission, and clearing one does not clear the other.** §4 governs whether reading and privately logging archive interviews is permitted at all. This section governs something that only becomes relevant much later: whether any StoryCorps-derived case may ever move toward public form.

**Standing commitment, made directly to StoryCorps in this project's own research access request:** no distribution, publication, or public use of any StoryCorps-derived material, in any form, without first submitting a separate, specific request to StoryCorps for that use. This is not a self-imposed caution to be revisited later — it is a stated commitment to the archive itself, and it holds regardless of how thoroughly a case has cleared this project's own internal Publication & Anonymization Standard.

**Practical effect:** even a StoryCorps-sourced case that clears every internal gate — Checklist, Comparison Protocol, full compositing under the Publication Standard — is not yet publishable. It requires one additional, external step this project's own internal machinery cannot substitute for or waive on its own behalf. Any future session working toward publishing a StoryCorps-derived case should treat this as a hard stop requiring outreach to StoryCorps directly, not as a step to infer from context or assume already handled.
