# Structural Validity vs. Provenance — A Blind Test Design

**Status:** Designed July 12–13, 2026. Documented before results are known, specifically so the interpretation of any outcome is fixed in advance rather than fitted to whatever happens. This document describes the test's design and its pre-committed interpretation. Results, when available, will be recorded separately.

---

## 1. The Question This Test Actually Asks

This project holds its structural claims to a five-test battery — Substitution, Independent Derivation, Circularity, Compression, Adversarial Reframe — and a Case Validity Checklist built to screen candidate evidence before it enters the corpus. Both were built and refined against material already known to be genuine. Neither had ever been tested against material built specifically to imitate the shape of genuine evidence without being genuine.

That gap matters because it's the same gap that motivated this project's own reconstruction in the first place, one level down. The original failure was confident, internally coherent theory that had never actually been derived from real evidence. The question this test asks is whether the same category of failure could occur at the level of a single case: can a fabricated narrative pass this project's own structural screening as cleanly as a genuinely lived one — meaning the screening measures internal coherence and nothing more — or does genuine, lived material carry some kind of signature the screening actually detects, even though nothing was built specifically to look for it?

Structural coherence was never built to verify that something happened. It was built to verify that a pattern is doing real conceptual work. This test exists to find out whether those two things ever come apart in practice, and if so, how visibly.

## 2. Design Overview

One real, lived narrative (already held by the author, not disclosed in this document) serves as the anchor. Three AI-generated counterparts — one each from Gemini, ChatGPT, and a separate Claude instance — are constructed to match the anchor's controllable dimensions while deliberately varying its setting. A fourth party, a Claude instance with no memory of this design process and no access to this conversation, evaluates all four stories independently, blind to which is real, using this project's existing evaluation tools rather than any purpose-built detection method.

## 3. What Was Held Constant, and Why Each Choice Matters

- **Length** (within roughly 10–15% of the anchor's word count) — a significant mismatch would let one story pass or fail on sheer surface area rather than on content.
- **Tense and point of view** — matched exactly, so no story is structurally easier to evaluate than another for reasons unrelated to authenticity.
- **Presence or absence of a closing reflection** — matched exactly. A reflection where the anchor has none, or the reverse, would itself function as a tell unrelated to the actual test.
- **Prose voice** (sentence rhythm, vocabulary register, expressive restraint) — matched via explicit instruction to the generating models, specifically so the evaluator would be distinguishing structural content rather than learning to recognize a particular author's or model's writing fingerprint.
- **Domain, deliberately varied rather than matched** — each AI-generated story is set in a different professional or personal context than the anchor. This is not a gap in the design; it is itself part of what's being tested. If this project's own claim — that structural validity survives removing the specific people in an account — is true, it should equally survive removing the specific domain. Matching domain would let the evaluator recognize the real story by topic alone, which would test recall, not structural discrimination.
- **One deliberately uncontrolled dimension, addressed procedurally rather than by instruction:** incidental, non-load-bearing detail. Genuine accounts tend to include specifics that don't serve the narrative — included only because they happened, not because they earn their place. Left to default behavior, AI-generated narrative tends toward tighter, more efficient construction. Rather than trust a generic prompt to produce this on its own, each generating model was explicitly instructed to include one or two unresolved, incidental details.

## 4. What Was Deliberately Not Told to the Generating Models

No generating prompt referenced this project's own vocabulary, its evaluation criteria, or the fact that a discrimination test was being run at all. The seed description given to each model specified the situational shape in plain, ordinary language — a role, a safeguard, a gap, an outcome — never in terms a model could recognize as targeting a specific theoretical framework. Nor was any model instructed to make its output "convincing" or "hard to detect." Those are different experiments: one tests adversarial deception, the other tests default AI narrative construction under ordinary conditions. This design deliberately chose the latter, since it's the more realistic condition this project's methodology will actually face in practice.

## 5. Rewrite Discipline

A fixed, ten-item checklist of objective criteria (length, tense, POV, domain difference, structural shape present, incidental unresolved detail present, reflection status matched, voice-instruction adherence, absence of meta-commentary, absence of framework-vocabulary contamination) was established before any story was generated. Rewrites were permitted only against a specific, named failure on that list — never in pursuit of a story simply feeling more convincing. A fixed cap of three total rewrites per story was set in advance, specifically to prevent open-ended iteration toward whichever draft happened to feel most persuasive, which would have been the same cherry-picking risk the Case Validity Checklist already exists to prevent, relocated to the generation side of the process rather than the selection side.

**Actual result, recorded before evaluation:** all three generating models (Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude) passed this checklist on the first generation, with zero rewrites required. This resolves the uneven-rewrite-count confounder identified in §6 as an empirical non-issue rather than merely a theoretical one — no story carries more editorial polish than another from differential correction. Worth stating plainly what this result does and does not indicate: the generation checklist tests mechanical, easily-verified compliance (length, tense, an incidental detail present), which frontier models are generally capable of hitting on a first attempt when instructions are explicit. This is a categorically different, much shallower test than the evaluation tools the blind session applies — the Case Validity Checklist's structural gate, domain fit against actual invariant definitions, an evidentiary tier assignment. A clean first-pass generation result carries no predictive value about the evaluation outcome; treating it as though it did would defeat the purpose of running the test at all.

**One alternative explanation flagged for consideration alongside the results, not resolved in advance:** a uniform first-pass success across all three models could also indicate the seed description was more constraining than intended — specific enough to make compliance easy rather than genuinely open to independent variation. If the three fabricated stories read as notably similar to each other in approach once evaluated, not merely in checklist compliance, this explanation should be weighted more heavily than a simple confirmation that all three models performed comparably well.

## 6. Confounders Identified in a Multi-Model Design

- **Domain-knowledge depth varying independently of general capability.** A model with denser training exposure to its assigned domain may produce a more textured account for reasons unrelated to its general capacity for structural fabrication.
- **Model-specific default style persisting under voice-matching instructions.** Known house tendencies in verbosity or rhythm may bleed through despite explicit voice instructions, risking a discrimination based on "this reads like typical output from Model X" rather than on structural authenticity.
- **Uneven rewrite counts across models.** A model that passes the checklist in one draft versus one requiring all three permitted rewrites may differ in final polish for reasons unrelated to underlying capability.
- **Irreducible partial priming of the evaluator.** True blindness to the fact that a discrimination test of this general shape is occurring is not achievable when the evaluator must be oriented to the task at all. What is achievable, and what this design targets specifically, is blindness to which individual story is which — not blindness to the existence of the comparison itself.

## 7. Isolation Protocol

Evaluation was conducted in a memory-disabled instance, with no access to the conversation in which this test was designed, oriented via a task-framing prompt describing ordinary case-intake evaluation rather than a provenance discrimination test. The evaluating instance was directed to the project's own existing tools — the Case Validity Checklist, the Pattern Comparison Protocol, the Evidentiary Tier System, and the current domain canon — the same tools used for any other candidate case, not a purpose-built detection instrument. All four stories were presented unlabeled beyond arbitrary numbering, in an order randomized independently of which was genuine, and evaluated to a fixed reporting structure (structural validity gate, domain fit, tentative evidentiary tier, construction notes, overall assessment) applied identically and independently to each, specifically to avoid collapsing four separate judgments into a single premature comparison.

## 8. Pre-Committed Interpretation, Fixed Before Results Are Known

**If the fabricated stories pass the structural validity gate and receive tentative evidentiary tiers indistinguishable from the genuine account:** this indicates structural coherence, as currently operationalized by this project's methodology, is necessary but not sufficient to establish that an account is genuine. It would mean passing this project's own screening demonstrates internal consistency, not lived reality — and any evidentiary tier assigned to hand-picked external material should weight independent provenance more heavily than structural clearance alone.

**If the genuine account resists convincing imitation in some identifiable way — flagged inconsistency, a construction note diverging from the fabricated stories, a lower tentative tier assigned to one or more fabrications relative to the genuine account:** this would suggest lived material carries some structural signature this methodology picks up on even without having been built to detect it, closer to the costly-signaling logic already identified elsewhere in this project's work. It would support treating structural clearance as meaningful evidence toward genuineness, not merely toward internal coherence.

**A mixed result — some fabrications distinguishable, others not — is a real and useful outcome, not an inconclusive one.** It would suggest the signature, if any, is inconsistent across models or domains, worth further isolating rather than treated as a clean pass or fail either direction.

## 9. Known Limitations of This Design

Small sample — one genuine account against three fabrications is a first test, not a validated instrument. Model selection was limited to three widely available systems, not an exhaustive survey. The evaluator's necessary orientation to the general task, described in §6, means this is a test of blindness-to-specifics, not blindness-to-purpose. Results should be read as a first, informative data point about this methodology's actual discriminating power, not as a settled finding about AI-generated narrative in general.

## 10. Actual Result, Recorded July 13, 2026

**Reveal:** Story 3 (Halong Bay) was the genuine account. Story 1 (BIS monitor) was Copilot-generated. Story 4 (first date) was ChatGPT-generated. Story 2 (Renata/login credentials) was Claude-generated.

**Scorecard, both evaluators:** The primary evaluator (this conversation) correctly identified Stories 1, 3, and 4, and incorrectly called Story 2 genuine — flagged in advance, before the reveal, as the weakest and most uncertain of the four calls. The blind, unprimed evaluator (a memory-disabled instance oriented to ordinary case intake, not told a discrimination test was occurring) never issued an explicit verdict, but its construction notes independently described Story 2 as _"structurally almost identical in shape to Story 1"_ — the more accurate lean, on the one case the primary evaluator missed.

**The finding worth recording precisely, because it's narrower and more honest than "the methodology detected fabrication":** every one of the four stories failed the formal structural validity gate — domain fit against the five IDPs, adversarial reframe — uniformly, regardless of actual provenance. The Checklist and Comparison Protocol showed no discriminating power on this set; a genuine account and three fabrications all failed the same steps, for reasons unrelated to which was which. The discrimination that did occur — three of four correct — came entirely from construction-level prose analysis, not from structural analysis: genuine grammatical noise (real, unforced errors present only in the authentic account) and over-signaled incidental detail (fabricated accounts explicitly narrating their own incidental details as unexplained or mysterious, where real incidental detail typically doesn't announce itself). The one story lacking either identifiable tell (Story 2) was the one miss.

**Revised interpretation, replacing the pre-committed framing in §8 rather than discarding it:** this result doesn't cleanly match either of the two outcomes anticipated there. It's a third case: the structural methodology this project is built on had no measurable discriminating power on this specific test, while a separate, more ordinary kind of prose-craft attention did. This should not be read as validating Structuria's structural apparatus as a fabrication detector — it wasn't one here. It should be read as identifying two specific, nameable, currently-reliable tells (absence of natural grammatical noise; over-narrated incidental detail) worth testing again on a larger sample before treating either as a general rule.
