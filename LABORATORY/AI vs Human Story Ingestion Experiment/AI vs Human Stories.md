I had thought the favor was simple enough. A colleague in another service line needed help troubleshooting a telemetry dropout on one of the older BIS monitors, and I happened to be the one who’d worked on that unit during its last PM. It felt natural to step in — we’d shared enough cases and enough late‑afternoon hallway conversations that I assumed the gesture would land as a small sign of professional camaraderie. I even remembered to bring the spare mounting bracket I’d been meaning to return for weeks, the one with the tiny dent on the underside that nobody could explain.

But when I arrived in the ICU, he barely looked up from his workstation. He handed me the work order without context, like I was just another tech passing through. I walked through the usual checks — cabling, network switch, the flaky port we’d flagged last quarter — and resolved the issue in under fifteen minutes. When I told him it was fixed, he nodded once, already typing something unrelated. No “thanks,” no acknowledgment that I’d rearranged my morning to get there before rounds. He did mention, offhand, that the coffee machine on 3B was dispensing half portions again, though I’m still not sure why.

I left the unit feeling oddly disoriented. The favor hadn’t been grand, but I’d expected… something. Recognition, maybe. Or at least the sense that the gesture meant what I thought it meant. Instead, it felt like I’d misread the entire relationship, like I’d projected closeness where there was only convenience.

I’ve been turning it over since then. Maybe I offered more than the situation could hold. Maybe it was nothing at all. I’m not sure yet, and I’m letting that uncertainty sit for now.

I met him for our first date at a small coffee shop that had a crooked chalkboard menu and a jar of mismatched stir sticks by the register. We talked for nearly two hours, long enough that I started to feel an unusual sense of ease around him. Not closeness exactly, but something that seemed to be moving in that direction.

When we left, he realized he had misplaced his wallet. We retraced our steps along the block, checked the shop, and looked under a bench with peeling green paint. He seemed embarrassed more than worried. Since he needed to get across town and had no cash or cards, I offered to cover his train fare and buy him a quick dinner before he headed home. It felt like the obvious thing to do. I remember thinking that if I were in his position, I would have appreciated the gesture.

He accepted, but something changed afterward. During dinner, he became quieter. When I tried to make light conversation, he answered politely but briefly. At one point, he thanked me again, and the way he said it sounded formal, almost distant. I had expected the favor to create a sense of connection or at least mutual warmth. Instead, it seemed to place an awkward weight between us.

A week later, he sent me a message repaying the exact amount. Not rounded up, not rounded down. Exact. He also said he had enjoyed meeting me but did not think we were a match.

I was surprised by how much that bothered me. The money itself never mattered. What unsettled me was realizing that the meaning I attached to the favor had existed mostly in my own mind. Looking back, I still do not think helping him was a mistake. I am just less certain than I once was about what kindness means to the person receiving it.

I covered Renata's territory for two weeks while her mother was in the hospital in Phoenix. I hadn't been asked to—our regional manager mentioned she was drowning, and I just started forwarding her case reports to myself and driving out to her accounts after my own calls. One of her hospitals was doing a valve replacement evaluation and needed same-day in-service on the new catheter system, so I did that too, on a Thursday, still wearing the lanyard from a conference the week before that I never took off.

I thought Renata and I were close in the way you get close with someone you've shared a car with at 6 a.m. for training seminars, someone whose kid's name you know. So when she came back, I expected—I don't know. Not thanks exactly. Some acknowledgment that I'd kept her numbers from cratering while her world was falling apart.

Instead she asked why I'd used my own login credentials on her accounts instead of routing everything through her, since now the quarterly attribution looked "confusing." She said it flatly, in the break room, while microwaving something that smelled like burnt popcorn even though it clearly wasn't popcorn. She wasn't unkind about it. She just wanted the record straightened out.

I told her I'd flag it with our ops coordinator, and I did, and it got fixed within a day. We still work together fine. She sent me a case referral last month, actually, unprompted, which I took as its own kind of answer, though not the one I'd been waiting for.

I keep thinking about the credentials thing. Not because it wounded me, exactly. More because I still can't tell if I misjudged how close we were, or if closeness just doesn't transfer the way I assumed it would once something is on the line.
