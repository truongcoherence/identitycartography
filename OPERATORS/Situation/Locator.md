
# **LOCATOR**

Locator is the **Situation Operator** that reveals **positional geometry** — where the situation is located within its environmental structure.  
- It does **not** describe identity.  
- It does **not** describe motivation.  
- It does **not** describe preference.

Locator exposes **positional constraint**.

---

## **1. What Locator Is**

Locator answers:

> **“Where is this situation positioned within its environment?”**

It reveals:

- positional asymmetry  
- structural advantage or disadvantage  
- environmental bias  
- local constraint distribution  
- the geometry shaping possible movement  

- Locator does **not** interpret identity.  
- Locator does **not** evaluate decisions.  
- Locator does **not** describe psychology.

Locator describes **environmental position**, not **personal position**.

---
## **2. Domain Alignment — Topophysics**

Locator is anchored to **Topophysics**, the physics of contextual geometry.

Topophysics defines:

- **positional asymmetry** — some positions carry more constraint  
- **local bias** — environments push identity toward certain trajectories  
- **constraint distribution** — pressure is not evenly spread  
- **structural gradients** — movement is easier in some directions  
- **contextual geometry** — shape of the environment determines possibility  

From your Topophysics canon:

> “Topophysics governs contextual geometry… the positional asymmetries that shape possibility.”  

Locator is the operator that makes these asymmetries visible.

---

## **3. What Locator Reveals (Structural Output)**

Locator produces **positional readings** — not judgments, not advice.

### **High Position**  
The situation sits in a structurally advantageous location.  
Constraint is low; movement is easy.

### **Low Position**  
The situation sits in a structurally disadvantaged location.  
Constraint is high; movement is costly.

### **Tilted Position**  
Environmental geometry biases movement in one direction.

### **Pinned Position**  
The situation is held in place by local constraint.

### **Floating Position**  
The situation is not anchored; movement is unpredictable.

### **Cross‑Current Position**  
Environmental forces push against intended direction.

These readings describe **environmental geometry**, not identity behavior.

---

## **4. Activation Conditions — When Locator Matters**

Locator activates when:

- position shapes possibility  
- constraint is unevenly distributed  
- environmental bias is present  
- movement is restricted by geometry  
- the situation is “stuck” or “tilted”  
- local forces push identity toward specific outcomes  
- positional asymmetry creates friction  

Topophysics states:

> “Context determines the distribution of constraint.”  

Locator reveals that distribution.

---

## **5. Boundaries — What Locator Cannot Do**

Locator does **not**:

- describe identity  
- describe emotion  
- describe intention  
- describe motivation  
- prescribe movement  
- evaluate choices  
- predict outcomes  
- interpret meaning  

Locator is **structural**, not psychological.

Topophysics explicitly forbids psychological interpretation:

> “It does not govern identity, preference, or behavior.”  

Locator inherits this boundary.

---

## **6. Distortions — How Locator Is Misread**

Locator has two canonical distortions:

### **Position as Merit**  
Mistaking positional advantage for personal capability.

### **Constraint as Failure**  
Mistaking positional disadvantage for personal error.

These distortions arise from misreading **positional geometry**, not identity.

---

## **7. Public Summary (Reader‑Safe)**

Locator reveals **where a situation is positioned** within its environmental geometry — its advantages, disadvantages, biases, and constraints.  
It shows whether the situation is tilted, pinned, floating, or cross‑current.  
Locator does not describe identity or psychology; it describes **positional structure**.
