# Preamble

#### Scope:  
Cohesion governs the internal stability of identity once it becomes a multi‑part structure carrying shared load. It describes how identity maintains continuity when obligations, roles, and internal components must support one another to sustain the future selected by Judgment and protected by Boundaries. Cohesion does not govern harmony, unity, emotional closeness, teamwork, or interpersonal connection. It governs the physics of interdependence inside the membrane.

#### Intent:  
The purpose of this canon is to describe the mechanics by which identity distributes load, stabilizes weak links, and prevents instability from propagating across internal components. Cohesion exists to ensure that identity remains structurally intact when the demands of a chosen future require multiple internal parts to work together. This canon explains the forces, architecture, and distortions that shape internal stability under shared load, and establishes Cohesion as the third Identity Domain Physics.

#### Guardrails:  
This canon is descriptive, not prescriptive. It does not evaluate personality, emotional intelligence, communication skill, or relational ability. It does not offer advice on collaboration, teamwork, or conflict resolution. Cohesion is not unity, belonging, or harmony. It is a structural system that activates only when identity must sustain shared load. Interpret this canon as identity physics, not psychology or self‑help.

# 1. Definition & Domain

Cohesion is the system that maintains internal stability once identity becomes a multi‑component structure responsible for carrying shared load. It governs the physics of interdependence, where the continuity of the whole depends on the integrity and coordination of its parts. Cohesion describes how identity organizes itself internally so that no single component becomes a point of catastrophic failure.

Cohesion does not express emotional closeness, unity, or agreement.  
It expresses load‑bearing integration.

Cohesion does not optimize for efficiency or minimal redundancy.  
It creates stability through distributed capacity, ensuring that shocks, demands, and obligations do not concentrate in ways that exceed the tolerance of any single part.

Its function is to:

- integrate internal parts into a coherent, mutually supportive architecture
    
- distribute load across components to prevent concentration and collapse
    
- identify and stabilize weak links before instability propagates
    
- maintain continuity under sustained, uneven, or compounding pressure
    
- preserve a shared frame so that all parts operate from the same orientation
    

Cohesion is the third IDP because once a future is chosen (Judgment) and its conditions are regulated (Boundaries), identity must still hold itself together under the load that remains.

# 2. Internal Architecture

Cohesion is the first IDP where identity is no longer a point or a membrane — it is a system composed of interacting parts. These parts may include:

- roles
    
- commitments
    
- values
    
- competencies
    
- identities
    
- obligations
    
- internalized expectations
    

A “part” is any internal component that carries load independently yet contributes to the stability of the whole.

a. Parts create structure  
Once identity has parts, it has:

- joints
    
- seams
    
- interfaces
    
- dependencies
    

These are the locations where load transfers and where failure can propagate.

b. Parts require coordination  
Interdependence means:

- no part can operate in isolation
    
- no part can carry unlimited load
    
- no part can fail without affecting others
    

Cohesion governs this coordination.

c. Load moves through parts  
Load does not remain where it originates.  
It travels:

- across roles
    
- across commitments
    
- across internal expectations
    

This movement is the basis of propagation.

d. Architecture determines resilience  
A well‑integrated architecture:

- distributes load
    
- dampens shocks
    
- isolates weak links
    

A poorly integrated architecture:

- concentrates load
    
- amplifies shocks
    
- spreads instability
    

Cohesion is the physics of this architecture.

# 3. Forces of Cohesion

Cohesion operates according to four structural forces derived from your invariants.

## a. Structural Interdependence Is Required

Cohesion only exists when parts rely on one another.  
If parts can operate independently, the domain does not activate.

Interdependence is not optional — it is the entry condition.

## b. Load Must Be Distributed

Concentrated load creates instability.  
Distributed load creates resilience.

Distribution is not fairness — it is physics.

## c. Weak Links Propagate Instability

A single fragile component can destabilize the entire system.  
Weak links are not moral flaws — they are structural vulnerabilities.

## d. Cohesion Rests on a Shared Frame

Parts must operate from a common reference:

- shared meaning
    
- shared assumptions
    
- shared orientation
    

Without a shared frame, integration collapses into drift.

These forces constitute the physics of Cohesion.

# 4. Propagation Dynamics

Propagation is the defining behavior of multi‑part identity.

## a. Load Propagation

Load moves from one part to another:

- stress in one role increases stress in another
    
- failure in one obligation increases pressure elsewhere
    
- instability in one domain spreads
    

Propagation is not emotional contagion — it is structural transfer.

## b. Instability Propagation

Weak links amplify instability:

- a fragile part cannot absorb load
    
- load spills into adjacent parts
    
- adjacent parts destabilize
    
- the system cascades
    

Propagation is exponential, not linear.

## c. Redundancy Dampens Propagation

Redundancy is not inefficiency — it is shock absorption.

Redundant capacity:

- absorbs excess load
    
- slows propagation
    
- prevents collapse
    

## d. Frame Drift Propagates Misalignment

If parts operate from different assumptions:

- coordination fails
    
- load distribution becomes uneven
    
- propagation accelerates
    

## e. Propagation Is Predictable

Propagation follows structural pathways:

- high‑load parts
    
- weak interfaces
    
- misaligned frames
    

Cohesion models these pathways to prevent collapse.

Cohesion also shapes how identity models load movement.

## a. Distribution Modeling

Identity evaluates how load should be shared across parts.

## b. Propagation Forecasting

Identity anticipates how instability will spread if unaddressed.

## c. Redundancy Accounting

Identity tracks where overlap is necessary to prevent collapse.

## d. Failure Simulation

Identity simulates how the system would fail under stress.

These mechanics describe how identity computes interdependence.

# 7. Behavioral Signatures

Cohesion expresses itself externally in predictable patterns.

## a. Distributed Effort

Identity spreads load across multiple internal or external supports.

## b. Redundancy Preservation

Identity maintains backup capacity rather than optimizing everything to zero slack.

## c. Weak‑Link Stabilization

Identity reinforces fragile components before they propagate instability.

## d. Shared‑Frame Enforcement

Identity ensures all parts operate from the same rules, meaning, and orientation.

## e. Smooth Internal Transitions

Identity shifts between roles, tasks, or states without fracture.

# 8. Failure Modes

Cohesion collapses in predictable ways.

## a. Fragmentation

Parts disconnect, and load can no longer be shared.

## b. Load Concentration

Too much weight falls on a single component, leading to collapse.

## c. Propagation Cascades

Instability spreads across parts faster than the system can absorb.

## d. Frame Drift

Parts operate from incompatible assumptions, causing misalignment.

## e. Redundancy Loss

Optimization removes slack, making the system fragile.
