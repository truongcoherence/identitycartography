**Distinct from two adjacent documents, on purpose:**

- Each domain's own **Provenance Notes** table (e.g., Judgment 2.3) shows current status at a glance, but not the argument behind a status change.
- The **Second Audit** document (July 13, 2026) is a closed, dated snapshot — a written account of one specific audit pass. ---

## What This Kind of Audit Is, and What It Isn't

There are now three structurally different kinds of finding this project has produced, and conflating any of them understates — or overstates — what's actually been checked.

**Bedrock** (the original good case, and the standard everything else is measured against): a claim whose every required structural element is verifiable directly from the written record, with no gap, no unstated reliance on memory, and no ambiguity a competing account could exploit. This is what "held up completely under the new standard" means in practice — not "plausible," not "consistent with," but checked element-by-element and clean. Bedrock is the target state, not the default assumption.

**Aether** (the first audit's finding, July 9–10, 2026): claims with no evidentiary substance behind them at all — internally coherent, well-formed, and never actually derived from real evidence. The appropriate response is retraction.

**Fog** (this audit's characteristic finding): real material, genuinely present, not yet resolved into a form that can be checked with full confidence — a documentation gap or an unresolved ambiguity, not an absence of terrain. Fog is not evidence the underlying work was hollow; it's evidence a standard introduced _after_ a domain's restoration is now being applied retroactively, and is capable of finding real gaps on material this project had already approved. The appropriate response to fog is not retraction — it's closer attention: locate the raw material, re-check it against the current standard, and either resolve the ambiguity or downgrade the status honestly.

A single domain's audit can surface all three kinds of finding at once, and each entry below should say which it is. Bedrock findings matter as much as Aether or Fog ones — a running log that only records problems will read as more damning than the actual state of the project, since it silently omits the material that already cleared the bar.

## Methodology

For each constraint or invariant already carrying a source case:

1. **Isolate the claim.** State exactly what the constraint or invariant asserts — no more, no less.
2. **Check the written record against the claim**, not against what the author remembers but didn't write down (per the Case Validity Checklist's rule that self-authored cases don't get privileged access to unwritten memory).
3. **Run Adversarial Reframe**: can the case as written be fully explained by a competing, mundane account that doesn't require the claim at all? If yes, the case doesn't uniquely establish the claim, regardless of how well it otherwise fits.
4. **Resolve one of two ways:**
    - **Resolve** — a detail in the record (or one that can honestly be added) disambiguates the two readings. Revise the write-up to make it explicit; status stays Restored.
    - **Cannot resolve** — nothing in the written record settles it. Status moves from Restored to **Held, not confirmed**, distinct from Retired (the claim isn't shown false, just unproven as written) and distinct from Restored (it isn't cleanly confirmed either).
5. **Classify the finding as Bedrock, Fog, or Aether** (see above) before logging it. Bedrock findings should be logged with the same discipline as problems — this log is not only a problem list. Aether findings are rare at this stage — most restored domains already survived the first audit — and should be treated as more serious than a Fog finding of the same surface severity.

A finding logged here does not by itself change canon. It's the input to a decision, and the decision — once made — gets logged in the same entry, not left implicit.
