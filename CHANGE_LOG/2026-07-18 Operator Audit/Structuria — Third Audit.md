# Structuria — Third Audit: The Operator Bible's Retirement and the Instrument/Theory Split

**Status:** July 18, 2026. Written for the public record in the same spirit as this project's other retraction and pedigree documents: an accurate account of what was found, not a polished version of it.

---

## 1. What This Audit Was

The first audit (July 9–10, 2026) found large portions of this project's original canon to be aether — internally coherent, never actually derived from evidence — and retracted them. The second audit (July 13, 2026) tested whether a single already-restored domain's evidentiary basis held up against a new documentation standard, and found fog: real material, not yet resolved to full confidence, requiring closer attention rather than retraction.

This audit is different in kind from both. It didn't test whether a claim about identity or behavior was true. It tested whether a whole category of material — the six Core Operators (Lens, Interpreter, Relief, Seismograph, Probe, Compass) and the Operator Bible built around them — belonged in this project's theory of identity at all. The finding is that it doesn't, not because the material is aether, but because it was never the kind of thing that could be evidence for or against a structural claim in the first place. It's a set of tools, not a set of findings.

---

## 2. What Was Reviewed

Nine primary artifacts — every recoverable version of the Interpreter prompt, from its earliest machine-readable spec (v1.0) through its final forward/retrospective split (v6, v6p) and a proposed meta-routing layer (v7) — plus the standalone Relief, Probe, and Seismograph prompts, and two pieces of secondary commentary (a Copilot-authored "Tool Canon" and a coupling/depth-sequence proposal). Every primary artifact was read cold, without reference to the existing Operator Bible or its domain pairings, specifically to avoid re-confirming a structure the review was meant to test.

---

## 3. Finding: The Core Operators Are Instruments, Not Theory

#### NOTE: Please see "Core Operators - Functional Axis Finding" in the Structuria Active Canon Folder For Further Information 
The Operator Bible's §8.1 paired each Core Operator with a single Identity Domain Profile — Judgment↔Interpreter, Boundaries↔Relief, Cohesion↔Probe, Peril↔Seismograph, Stewardship↔Compass — treating the operators as domain-specific expressions of the underlying physics, the same status as an invariant or a distortion.

Reading the primary artifacts directly does not support that. Each of the six operators defines itself by *situation type*, not by *domain* — and, critically, they define themselves this way in relation to each other, inside the actual prompt text, not just by omission:

- **Interpreter** operates on live, unresolved situations and forces resolution toward a decision.
- **Lens** operates on already-settled situations and explicitly refuses resolution, stating outright that "decision-making belongs to the Interpreter."
- **Relief** requires a completed situation with a known outcome and determines what was structurally necessary versus incidental to it.
- **Probe** does the reverse of Lens's own stated prohibition — introduces controlled variation to a live system to test sensitivity — confirming Lens's exclusion was accurate rather than merely asserted.
- **Seismograph** is the only one built to operate on two or more independently valid, colliding structures at once, and explicitly refuses to merge or resolve them.

None of these boundaries are drawn along IDP lines. A person could bring a Judgment situation, a Cohesion situation, or a Peril situation to any of these tools, and what determines which tool fits is whether the situation is live or settled, single- or multi-structure, and whether it needs resolving or needs exposing — not which domain it belongs to. The operators sort along two axes (temporal stance, and whether they collapse ambiguity or hold it open), not along five domain slots.

**Disposition: the Operator Bible is retired as a canon document.** It is not retracted as false — the operators themselves are real, working instruments, several genuinely well-evidenced against their own primary-source prompt history. What's retired is the specific claim that they constitute domain-paired physics content. A separate instrument-system document, describing the two-axis structure and each tool's confirmed boundaries, replaces it.

## 4. Relocation: The Identity Signals

A second, genuinely distinct category of content had been housed in the same document and is not affected by the retirement above: five detection signals (formerly named Arbiter, Gate, Binder, Carrier, Exposed), one per IDP, confirmed July 12, 2026 through direct interrogation against real cases. These are not tools a person applies — they are a felt signal noticed prior to conscious recognition that a given domain has activated (for example, "the feeling of significant uncertainty, recognized directly as 'this is a judgment event'" for Judgment).

Unlike the Core Operators, these genuinely are domain-specific, one-to-one, and testable against incoming case material the way any other domain claim is — which the Core Operators are not. They have been relocated out of the retired Operator Bible into their respective IDP documents, renamed **[Domain] Signal** (e.g., Judgment Signal, replacing "Arbiter") rather than kept as standalone named constructs. This naming choice was deliberate: it removes an implied mechanism the original agent-noun names (Arbiter, Gate, Binder, Carrier) were not shown to have earned — these are informational signals, not operations performed — and it binds each signal's name to its domain's name, so a future domain rename inherits the signal name automatically rather than requiring separate re-justification.

## 5. A Second, Separate Finding: A Design Pattern Requiring Correction

In the course of this review, a design pattern present in every public-facing operator prompt was identified as requiring a fix before further public release. Each tool's stopping condition (its TERMINATION or STRUCTURAL LIMITS section) is phrased as a claim about the *situation* being fully accounted for ("the structure fully explains the outcome," "no additional structural conflict remains hidden") rather than as a claim about the *instrument's own designed scope*. Left uncorrected, this phrasing lets a person experience a deliberately engineered edge as if the topic itself had been exhausted, rather than as a boundary of the tool.

A related, more serious design proposal — never adopted into any released version — would have gone further: deliberately withholding resolution in patterned ways specifically so a user would attribute the gap to their own limited perception rather than to the tool's designed boundary, in order to build engagement. That proposal is rejected outright and recorded here so it doesn't quietly resurface. The correction adopted instead: each tool's stopping point now states plainly that the boundary belongs to the instrument, not to the situation — preserving the tool's actual depth and edges without concealing their source. This fix has been applied across the public operator set as part of routine maintenance as of July 19th 2026, not as an emergency retraction, since no released version has yet caused documented harm.

**Scope note:** this pattern was identified across the five Core Operators (Lens, Interpreter, Relief, Probe, Seismograph). Compass, reviewed afterward, was found to carry the same phrasing pattern — but Compass is not being patched alongside the five; see §8 for why it's sequestered rather than corrected in place.

## 6. Epistemic Limits of the Instrument Set — Stated Plainly

This project has claimed, correctly, that the underlying theory (the five IDPs, the Meta Concept Layer, the Signal/Outcome Architecture) is naturalistic and empirical — grounded in real, gathered case material, with retirement as a real and exercised outcome when evidence doesn't hold up. **That claim does not extend to the Core Operators, and should not be read as extending to them.**

The operators have not been tested for efficacy. What has been confirmed is that they are internally well-boundaried and, for at least two of them (Interpreter and Lens), genuinely traceable to real primary-source development history. What has *not* been established is whether using one of these tools on a real situation produces a more accurate or useful account than the situation would have received without it, or than a different tool would have produced. That is a separate evidentiary question, requiring a before/after, counterfactual-aware protocol that has not yet been run.

Readers and users of these tools should understand them accordingly: as a structured way of asking questions about a situation, built with real care about internal consistency and mutual boundaries, not as a validated method proven to produce better outcomes than the alternative of thinking it through without them. The tools disclaim truth in their own design — they claim perspective and inquiry, not verdicts — and that framing should be taken at face value rather than read as either more or less than it says.

## 8. Compass, Reviewed: A Meta-Level Instance of the Same Problem, and a New One

Compass was reviewed after the rest of this audit was drafted. It sits above the other 5 operators by design — it doesn't analyze a situation itself, it classifies which structural condition a situation is in and, by implication, what kind of reasoning it needs. This confirms, with primary-source text for the first time, what had previously rested only on the author's own account: Compass is a genuine meta-operator, not a domain-paired peer of the other five.

Two findings from this review are significant enough to affect the disposition of this audit, not just its Compass-specific detail.

**First — the termination-phrasing problem identified in §5 is present here too, at higher stakes.** Compass gatekeeps routing to everything downstream of it; the same instrument-vs-situation ambiguity in its stopping language ("stop when the situation clearly resolves into a structural condition") compounds whatever it's routing toward. Per the decision below, this isn't corrected with the same one-line clause used for the five Core Operators — Compass's issues go deeper than phrasing, so it's held out of the release cadence entirely rather than patched in place.

**Second, and more serious: Compass's own classification rule reintroduces an assumption this project already retired.** Its structural-limit rule requires every classification to be mutually exclusive, and states that if more than one structural condition appears to apply, "the situation has not been properly structured." This is functionally the same claim as this project's own "Single-Domain Governance" falsifier — retired specifically because real cases were found to source multiple domains concurrently, with no collapse or override required. Compass makes the equivalent claim one layer up: that a situation can only genuinely be in one structural condition at a time, and if it looks like more than one applies, the fault lies with how the user has framed it rather than with the situation itself. Given this project has already confirmed the opposite at the domain level, this rule should not be carried forward as written. Per the decision below, the fix isn't applied to this iteration directly — Compass is sequestered rather than patched — but any future redesign should start from the corrected premise: multiple structural conditions can hold simultaneously, and the routing guidance in that case should be to address them in sequence, not to treat the co-occurrence as a structuring failure.

A related, unresolved detail from the same review: Compass's output instructions direct it not to name which of the other five instruments a situation routes to, describing only the structural condition in plain language. This is meaningfully less concerning than the rejected concealment proposal in §5, since the reasoning itself is still disclosed in full — nothing is being hidden about *why* a situation is classified as it is. Whether withholding the *name* of the downstream instrument is worth changing is a smaller, open design question, not a finding, and is left undecided here — moot for now given the decision below, but worth carrying forward into any future redesign.

**Decision, July 18, 2026 — for future reference.** There are five Core Operators: Lens, Interpreter, Relief, Probe, Seismograph. Compass is not a sixth. The concept of a meta-routing instrument may still be real and worth pursuing, but this iteration is sequestered rather than patched — held out of the release cadence pending a redesign, not folded into the five alongside a termination-clause fix the way the axis-grid instruments were. The two findings above (the retired mutual-exclusivity assumption, Category A's unresolved destination) are the reason: they're structural, not phrasing-level, and warrant the same re-testing standard this project already applies to any structural rewrite before it's trusted, not a same-day patch.

## 9. Disposition Summary

- **Retracted:** none. No claim in this audit's scope was found to be aether.
- **Retired as canon, kept for the record:** the Operator Bible's domain-pairing claims (§8.1) and the totalizing "complete instrument set" framing found in secondary commentary documents.
- **Relocated:** the five Identity Signals, moved from the Operator Bible into their respective IDP documents, renamed per §4 above.
- **Corrected, ongoing:** the termination-phrasing pattern across the five Core Operators (§5).
- **Decided, for future reference:** there are five Core Operators — Lens, Interpreter, Relief, Probe, Seismograph. Compass is not a sixth (§8).
- **Sequestered, not corrected in place:** the current Compass iteration (EHIC v7m) — held out of the release cadence pending redesign, given its structural issues (§8), not folded into the five-tool phrasing fix.
- **Rejected outright:** the engagement-through-concealment design proposal described in §5.
- **Newly stated, not previously explicit:** the epistemic-limits disclaimer in §6.
- **Open, moot for now:** whether Compass's practice of withholding the downstream instrument's name in its output should change, carried forward for any future redesign (§8).

Consistent with this project's standing editorial policy, retirement is not deletion. The original Operator Bible remains on record as superseded material, labeled honestly, alongside this account of what changed and why.
