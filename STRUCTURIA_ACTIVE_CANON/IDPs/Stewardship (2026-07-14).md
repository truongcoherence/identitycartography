# Stewardship

### Public Canon, Working Draft

**Status:** Work in progress, labeled as such deliberately. See the Judgment public canon document for the full explanation of the confidence labels used throughout this series. As a quick reference:

- **Established** — more than one independently-sourced observation, checked against competing explanations, holding up.
- **Supported, verification in progress** — real observational grounding exists; formal transcription and independent re-checking are underway.
- **Provisional** — a single observation; worth taking seriously, not yet load-bearing.
- **Conceptual only** — a clearly stated idea with no linked field observation yet. Not a discarded claim — just untested so far.

A claim that sounded coherent but was later found to have never actually been derived from real observation does exist elsewhere in this project's history — where that happened, the claim was retired outright, not published in any form. Nothing below is in that category.

---

## 1. What Stewardship Is

Stewardship is the ongoing, often uncompensated responsibility that accumulates around someone who has reliably held something together over time — a responsibility that can outlast the formal authority or title that originally came with it.

---

## 2. Invariants of Stewardship

### a. Stewardship Persists Past Formal Authority — _Supported, verification in progress_

A person can retain real, functioning responsibility for something well after any official role or title that would formally justify it has ended or changed.

_Grounding:_ Two primary cases, plus two independent contrast cases used to test the boundary of the claim — both involving long-tenured individuals whose responsibilities narrowed considerably over time, with no visible strain produced when they eventually left or retired. Those contrast cases support the idea that stewardship has to be _demonstrated_, not just accumulated through tenure alone — though one competing explanation hasn't yet been fully addressed in writing: that a low-strain departure near the end of a long tenure could also be explained by ordinary planned succession, independent of whether the person's reliability was ever genuinely load-bearing. That alternative looks less likely given the extended timeframes involved in both contrast cases, but it hasn't been explicitly ruled out yet, and this document says so rather than treating the claim as fully closed.

### b. Demonstrated Competence Compounds Into Uncompensated Obligation — _Provisional_

Once someone has visibly, repeatedly demonstrated competence in a given area, an informal expectation tends to form that they'll continue handling it — without a corresponding formal update to their role, compensation, or authority.

_Grounding:_ One case currently on record, showing a complete arc from an initial demonstration of competence through to an accumulated, unofficial expectation. A single account is enough to state the claim clearly but not enough to call it confirmed; a second, independent instance would move this forward.

### c. Continuity Without Integrity Degrades — _Provisional, with a distinct caveat_

Maintaining continuity in a role or responsibility, without also maintaining the internal integrity of how that continuity is held, leads to degradation over time even if the outward continuity itself never breaks.

_Grounding:_ Unusually, the only source currently informing this claim is self-referential — this very project's own internal review process serving as its own test case, rather than an external field observation. That's a categorically different kind of evidence than everything else in this document, and it's treated that way here: this claim needs a genuine, non-self-referential example before it's held with the same confidence as (a) or (b) above, and that need is stated plainly rather than left implicit.

### d. [Retired] — Stewardship Rests on Custodial Authority

An earlier version of this domain proposed that stewardship required some form of formal custodial authority to begin with. This was directly contradicted, not merely left unproven, by the same evidence supporting invariant (a) above — both of that invariant's source cases showed stewardship persisting in the clear absence of any custodial authority at all. It's retired outright rather than softened, since invariant (a) already covers the actual mechanism this one was reaching for.

## 3. Primitives of Stewardship

**None currently proposed.** A candidate was tested directly and found to belong to a more general, cross-domain concept still being developed and tested elsewhere in this framework, rather than to Stewardship specifically. No primitive is claimed here as a result, and no placeholder is used in its place.

## 4. Structural Distortions of Stewardship

**Hoarding as Preservation** — Holding onto responsibility or control past the point it's actually needed is mistaken for protecting something valuable, when it may just as easily be preventing a healthier redistribution of that responsibility.

**Optimization as Resilience** — Making a system more efficient is mistaken for making it more durable under strain. An optimized system and a resilient one are not the same thing, and improving one doesn't guarantee the other.

_A note on this section's status:_ as with the other domains in this series, these two distortions are conceptually well-formed but not yet tied to a dedicated field case of their own.

As with the other documents in this series, labels here will change as more material is checked — some claims will move toward _Established_, and it's possible some won't hold up and will be revised or retired instead, the way invariant (d) above already was. That's the intended mechanism, not a failure of it. This document will be updated as the work continues.
