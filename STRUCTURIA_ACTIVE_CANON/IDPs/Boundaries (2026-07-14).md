# Boundaries

### Public Canon, Working Draft

**Status:** Work in progress, labeled as such deliberately. See the Judgment public canon document for the full explanation of the confidence labels used throughout this series. As a quick reference:

- **Established** — more than one independently-sourced observation, checked against competing explanations, holding up.
- **Supported, verification in progress** — real observational grounding exists; formal transcription and independent re-checking are underway.
- **Provisional** — a single observation; worth taking seriously, not yet load-bearing.
- **Conceptual only** — a clearly stated idea with no linked field observation yet. This is different from a discarded claim — it just means the idea hasn't been tested against a real case yet, which is common for a newer part of the framework and isn't, on its own, a reason for concern.

A claim that sounded coherent but was later found to have never actually been derived from real observation does exist elsewhere in this project's history — where that happened, the claim was retired outright, not published in any form. Nothing below is in that category; the point of naming it here is the same as in the Judgment document — so "still being checked" and "was never real" don't get read as the same thing.

---

## 1. What Boundaries Are

Boundaries define the conditions that make a livable future actually possible, once Judgment has identified which futures those are. A boundary is not a wall in the simple sense — it's closer to a membrane: something that has to let some things through and stop others, or it isn't functioning as a boundary at all.

---

## 2. Invariants of Boundaries

### a. Membranes Are Semi-Permeable — _Conceptual only_

A functioning boundary always allows some flow and blocks some load. Total permeability (no boundary at all) and total blockage (no longer a boundary, now a wall) are both failure states, not stronger versions of the same idea.

_Grounding:_ This claim has not yet been tied to a dedicated field observation. It's carried forward as a clearly-stated concept, not yet as a tested one.

### b. Containment Is a Tax — _Supported, verification in progress_

Holding jurisdiction or expertise across more territory than a role formally requires has an ongoing cost — described here as a tax rather than a one-time toll, since it's paid continuously rather than once.

_Grounding:_ A primary case showing sustained fatigue from operating across multiple technical domains, deliberately tested against a contrasting case where a clear agreement about communication at the leadership level prevented the same fatigue from appearing — a real attempt to rule out a competing explanation, not just a single supporting anecdote.

### c. Misalignment Compounds — _Conceptual only_

Small mismatches between a person's identity and their environment tend to grow over time rather than stay fixed in size.

_Grounding:_ Only a general industry-level pattern (not a personal field case) has been checked for plausibility here, and this project's own evidentiary standards don't treat that kind of general corroboration as sufficient on its own. This is currently the least-grounded claim in this section and is labeled accordingly.

### d. The Bedrock Is Law — _Established_

Some limits are not up for negotiation or persuasion, regardless of how reasonably or persistently someone pushes against them.

_Grounding:_ The strongest evidentiary base in this domain — sourced across four different contexts (a personal relationship, a professional service relationship, a routine contracted repair, and an institutional employment decision), spanning relational, consumer, and workplace settings. Breadth across genuinely different contexts is harder to produce by accident than repetition within one context.

### e. Boundaries Require Jurisdictional Sorting — _Supported, verification in progress_

Before a boundary can be enforced, it first has to be correctly sorted into whose jurisdiction it actually falls under — misjudging that comes before any failure to hold the boundary itself.

_Grounding:_ One dedicated case involving an institutional-process authority dispute, plus two incidents already described under invariant (d) above. Worth stating plainly: the independent evidentiary count for this specific claim is closer to two than four, even though four cases are associated with it in total, since two are shared with the invariant above rather than separate confirmations.

## 4. Structural Distortions of Boundaries

**Line as Wall** — Mistaking a boundary for something that should never flex, when a boundary that never flexes under any condition has stopped functioning as a boundary at all.

**Enclosure as Security** — Mistaking the presence of a boundary for the guarantee of safety inside it. A boundary limits what can cross; it doesn't certify that everything already inside is safe.

_A note on this section's status:_ as with Judgment's distortions, these two are conceptually well-formed but not yet tied to a dedicated field case of their own — carried forward as clearly-stated claims, not yet independently field-tested ones.

**A related candidate, surfaced but not yet added:** a single account, evaluated elsewhere in this framework for an unrelated reason, pointed to a possible third distortion pattern in this domain — the reverse of the two above. Rather than a flexible thing being mistaken for something rigid, this pattern would describe a genuinely rigid, safety-critical rule being mistaken for something flexible and negotiable. This is currently a single-instance idea, not yet named formally or added to this document, consistent with how every other single-instance idea in this series is treated.
