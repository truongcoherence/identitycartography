# Structuria — 

### Public Canon, Working Draft

**Status:** Work in progress, labeled as such deliberately. See the Judgment public canon document for the full explanation of the confidence labels used throughout this series. As a quick reference:

- **Established** — more than one independently-sourced observation, checked against competing explanations, holding up.
- **Supported, verification in progress** — real observational grounding exists; formal transcription and independent re-checking are underway.
- **Provisional** — a single observation; worth taking seriously, not yet load-bearing.
- **Conceptual only** — a clearly stated idea with no linked field observation yet. Not a discarded claim — just untested so far.

**A note specific to this document:** this one is different from the five domain documents and the architecture document elsewhere in this series. It doesn't describe a domain of identity or a pipeline of action — it describes the three _kinds_ of claim this framework makes about any domain, and is honest about a significant early overclaim in how those three kinds of claim were originally described.

---

## 1. What These Three Terms Mean

**Invariants** are claims that recur across independently-sourced observations and would break something specific and checkable if removed. They're the closest thing this framework has to "the laws of a domain."

**Primitives** are meant to describe something narrower and more basic than an invariant: a felt, automatic operation a person performs, not a practiced skill requiring conscious, ongoing effort.

**Distortions** describe a specific, recurring way a real structural signal gets mistaken for something else — confusing the thing itself with something adjacent to it that's easier to notice.

---

## 2. Invariants: A Retired Overclaim, and What Actually Survived It

An earlier version of this framework claimed that every domain shared one identical underlying shape — the same four-part structure, appearing in the same order, across every domain, regardless of subject matter. On direct testing, that claim didn't survive: the uniform shape turned out to be the signature of a label applied evenly across domains after the fact, not something independently discovered in each one. It's been retired outright, not softened.

What replaced it, once each domain's invariants were checked against their own actual evidence rather than against a shared template, is considerably less tidy — and considerably more honest. Different domains in this framework currently have different numbers of invariants, built from different amounts and kinds of supporting material, because that's what each domain's real evidence turned out to support. That unevenness isn't a problem to smooth over; smoothing it over is exactly what produced the original, retracted claim.

What did survive, correctly: the basic definition of an invariant above — a claim that recurs across independent sources and would break something if removed — continues to hold up as the right way to think about this category. It was only the claim of a universal shared _shape_ across domains that failed.

---

## 3. Primitives: An Honest Finding of Zero, Not the Same as "None Exist"

Across every domain currently in this framework, not one previously proposed primitive has survived being checked directly against real, lived cases. Several were found to require ongoing conscious effort rather than the automatic quality a primitive is specifically supposed to have. Others had no supporting case at all. One candidate turned out to be real, but to belong to a more general concept spanning multiple domains rather than to the single domain it had been assigned to.

This is stated precisely because the natural but incorrect conclusion would be "primitives don't exist." That's not what was shown. What was shown is narrower: a specific set of proposed candidates didn't hold up once actually tested, in every domain checked so far. The category itself remains open — the one candidate that turned out to be real just wasn't where it was originally assumed to belong.

---

## 4. Distortions: Well-Formed, but Empirically Undertested — A Distinction This Framework Previously Blurred

Every named distortion across every domain in this framework passed an internal coherence check: each one does real conceptual work and doesn't lose its force when restated plainly. That result is genuine and hasn't been challenged.

What that result was previously allowed to imply, incorrectly, is something stronger: that distortions were also the most empirically confirmed content in the framework, tested against real cases the way the invariants above are. On direct checking against the accumulated case material, that's not currently true. The large majority of named distortions across this framework have no field case backing them at all yet — not disproven, simply untested against real evidence so far. A small handful have a first, light touch: one distortion has two independent light touches from two unrelated situations; a couple of others have exactly one. None has yet reached the bar this framework holds its invariants to before calling something confirmed.

This is named plainly, rather than left to be discovered later, because coherence-testing and evidence-testing are genuinely different kinds of validation, and earlier versions of this framework let the two read as equivalent when they aren't. A concept can be perfectly well-formed and still be waiting on real-world confirmation — that's not a contradiction, and it isn't a reason to distrust the concept. It's just an honest description of where the work currently stands.

**One additional finding worth naming:** a single account, evaluated for an unrelated reason elsewhere in this framework, surfaced a possible new distortion pattern not currently named anywhere — the reverse of an existing pair of named distortions in another domain (a genuinely rigid, safety-critical rule mistaken for something flexible and negotiable, rather than the more commonly named failure of mistaking something flexible for something rigid). This is held as a single-instance candidate, not yet added to the framework, consistent with how every other single-instance idea in this series is treated.

---

## 5. A Note on How to Read This Document

This document is, in a sense, about the framework's own honesty with itself: it exists specifically because an earlier, tidier version of these three categories didn't survive being checked directly, and the corrected version is less uniform and more provisional as a direct result. That's treated here as a sign the checking process works, not as a setback. As with the other documents in this series, some of what's described as undertested above will move toward stronger confidence as more material is checked, and it's possible some of it won't. This document will be updated as that work continues.
